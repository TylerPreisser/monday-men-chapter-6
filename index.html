Q01
Is a minister of the gospel liable for malpractice for using spiritual guidance rather than psychological or medical techniques?
Resolved — No Liability
Federal Status

Clergy malpractice has never been recognized by any U.S. court. Nally v. Grace Community Church (Cal. Supreme Court, 1988) ruled unanimously that imposing a standard of care on pastoral counseling would violate the Establishment Clause. Over 7,000 churches filed amicus briefs.

If a pastor holds a separate professional license (LPC, psychologist), they can be held to that profession's standard of care — not for pastoral counseling itself.
Key Cases
Nally v. Grace Community Church, 47 Cal.3d 278 (1988)

Q02
Can a Christian residence house in a college have the same standing as a fraternity/sorority for off-campus residency rules?
Largely Resolved — Yes in Principle
Federal Status

No single landmark case, but the viewpoint discrimination doctrine strongly favors equal treatment. A public university denying exemptions to a Christian house while granting them to Greek houses faces a strong First Amendment challenge.

Key precedents: Widmar v. Vincent (1981), Rosenberger v. UVA (1995), and Good News Club v. Milford (2001) establish that viewpoint-neutral access to facilities must include religious organizations.

Outcome is fact-specific — depends on whether the institution is public or private.
Key Cases
Widmar v. Vincent, 454 U.S. 263 (1981) Rosenberger v. UVA, 515 U.S. 819 (1995) Good News Club v. Milford (2001)
Q03
Can Christian high school students assemble on campus for religious discussion?
Resolved — Yes
Federal Status

The Equal Access Act of 1984 requires any public secondary school with a "limited open forum" to grant equal access to student religious groups. Board of Education v. Mergens (SCOTUS, 1990, 8-1) upheld the Act. Good News Club v. Milford (SCOTUS, 2001, 6-3) extended protection to elementary schools. Schools must annually certify compliance.

Key Cases
Equal Access Act of 1984 Board of Educ. v. Mergens, 496 U.S. 226 (1990) Good News Club v. Milford, 533 U.S. 98 (2001)
Q04
Can Christian teachers in public schools meet before class for prayer?
Resolved — Yes, With Boundaries
Federal Status

Kennedy v. Bremerton School District (SCOTUS, 2022, 6-3) — private religious expression during non-instructional time is protected. The ruling overruled the Lemon test. 2025 DOE guidance explicitly states teachers may pray during lunch or meet for prayer before school.

Teachers cannot lead students in prayer or use official authority to promote religion during school hours.
Key Cases
Kennedy v. Bremerton, 597 U.S. 507 (2022) DOE Guidance (2025)
Q05
Can Christian college students meet in groups on state university campus?
Resolved — Yes
Federal Status

Widmar v. Vincent (SCOTUS, 1981, 8-1) established the right. Rosenberger v. UVA (1995) extended protection to funding. InterVarsity v. University of Iowa (8th Cir., 2021) held that selective enforcement against religious groups is unconstitutional — officials were held personally liable.

Active tension: CLS v. Martinez (2010) allows "all-comers" policies, but selective enforcement against only religious groups is clearly illegal.
Key Cases
Widmar v. Vincent, 454 U.S. 263 (1981) Rosenberger v. UVA, 515 U.S. 819 (1995) InterVarsity v. Univ. of Iowa (8th Cir. 2021) CLS v. Martinez (2010)
Q06
Can HEW require a Bible college to admit drug addicts/alcoholics as "handicapped persons"?
Resolved — Depends on Federal Funding
Federal Status

No federal funds: Neither Section 504 nor the ADA applies. The ADA explicitly exempts religious organizations (42 U.S.C. §12187).

Any federal funds (including student aid) = Section 504 applies institution-wide per the Civil Rights Restoration Act of 1987. Current illegal drug users are excluded from protection (1992 amendments). Recovering addicts and alcoholics remain protected.

Key Cases
Section 504, Rehabilitation Act ADA Title III §12187 Civil Rights Restoration Act 1987 Grove City College v. Bell (1984)
Q07
Can a church build a religious school or day-care in a residential zone?
Resolved — Generally Yes
Federal Status

RLUIPA (2000) provides strict scrutiny protection. If secular schools or organizations are permitted in a zone, religious equivalents must receive equal treatment. Cutter v. Wilkinson (SCOTUS, 2005, unanimous) upheld RLUIPA. The DOJ enforces through its "Place to Worship Initiative."

Key Cases
RLUIPA, 42 U.S.C. §2000cc (2000) Cutter v. Wilkinson, 544 U.S. 709 (2005)
Q08
Can parents be prosecuted for sending kids to unapproved religious schools?
Largely Resolved — Generally No
Federal Status

Wisconsin v. Yoder (SCOTUS, 1972) established that Free Exercise can override compulsory education laws. All 50 states now permit homeschooling and private religious schooling in some form.

Q09
Is an independent religious school exempt from unemployment taxes like church-owned schools?
Resolved Against — No
Federal Status

St. Martin Evangelical Lutheran Church v. South Dakota (SCOTUS, 1981) — the FUTA exemption applies only to schools legally part of a church or "operated, supervised, controlled, or principally supported by" a church. Independent religious schools must pay unemployment taxes. Congress has not amended the statute in the decades since.

Key Cases
St. Martin v. South Dakota, 451 U.S. 772 (1981) DOL UIPL 28-87
Q10
Will the State enforce anti-discrimination laws against a church that fires a homosexual staff member?
In Flux — Partially Resolved
Federal Status

For ministers/religious function employees: NO enforcement — the ministerial exception applies (Hosanna-Tabor 2012, unanimous; Our Lady of Guadalupe 2020, 7-2).

For non-ministerial employees: UNSETTLED — Bostock v. Clayton County (2020) extended Title VII to sexual orientation but left open RFRA and Section 702 exemptions. Braidwood Management v. EEOC (5th Cir., 2023) held RFRA requires an exemption. SCOTUS has NOT directly ruled on the Bostock/RFRA intersection.

Q11
Can seminary trustees refuse to graduate a practicing homosexual?
Resolved — Yes
Federal Status

Four independent legal shields: (1) Ministerial exception (Hosanna-Tabor, Our Lady of Guadalupe). (2) Title VII Section 702 religious organization exemption. (3) Title IX religious exemption — upheld in Hunter v. DOE (9th Cir., 2024). (4) RFRA. The Fuller Seminary litigation (9th Cir., 2021) dismissed all claims after expulsion for same-sex marriage.

Key Cases
Hosanna-Tabor v. EEOC (2012) Our Lady of Guadalupe (2020) Hunter v. DOE (9th Cir. 2024)
Q12
Can a city continue its nativity scene in front of city hall?
Resolved — Almost Certainly Yes
Federal Status

Lynch v. Donnelly (SCOTUS, 1984) — nativity scene OK as part of a broader holiday display. American Legion v. AHA (SCOTUS, 2019, 7-2) established a "presumption of constitutionality" for longstanding displays. Kennedy v. Bremerton (2022) eliminated the Lemon test. Under the "historical practices" framework, a 40-year tradition is extremely strong.

Key Cases
Lynch v. Donnelly (1984) American Legion v. AHA (2019) Kennedy v. Bremerton (2022)
Q13
Can zoning laws prevent home Bible studies?
Largely Resolved — No, But Friction Persists
Federal Status

RLUIPA (2000) — if secular gatherings (poker nights, book clubs) are allowed, Bible studies cannot be singled out. Manhattan Beach, CA (2024) was forced to back down after ACLJ intervention.

The law is clear; enforcement is the issue. Local code enforcement officers often ignore or are unaware of federal protections.
Key Cases
RLUIPA (2000) Cutter v. Wilkinson (2005) ACLJ Manhattan Beach (2024)
Q14
Can a court decide which group gets the sanctuary in a church split?
Largely Resolved — Property Yes, Doctrine No
Federal Status

Serbian Eastern Orthodox Diocese v. Milivojevich (SCOTUS, 1976) — courts CANNOT decide matters of doctrine. Jones v. Wolf (SCOTUS, 1979) — courts CAN use "neutral principles" (deeds, corporate charters) to resolve property disputes without entangling in doctrine.

Active context: ~7,000 United Methodist churches disaffiliated 2019–2024. Litigation ongoing. Alabama Supreme Court (2025) rejected the UMC trust clause.

Q15
Must a religious school hire a practicing homosexual as a teacher?
Resolved — No
Federal Status

Three independent shields: (1) Ministerial exception — covers nearly all teachers at religious schools after Our Lady of Guadalupe (2020). (2) Title VII Section 702 religious organization exemption. (3) RFRA. Billard v. Charlotte Catholic (4th Cir., 2024) dismissed a teacher's claim after firing for announcing a same-sex engagement.

Key Cases
Hosanna-Tabor v. EEOC (2012) Our Lady of Guadalupe (2020) Billard v. Charlotte Catholic (4th Cir. 2024)
Q16
Can a church be fined for exuberant noise in worship?
Largely Resolved — Nuanced
Federal Status

A truly neutral noise ordinance CAN apply (Employment Division v. Smith, 1990). But RLUIPA (2000) provides strict scrutiny if the ordinance substantially burdens religious practice or discriminates. Reed v. Town of Gilbert (2015) — content-based distinctions receive strict scrutiny.

If a city allows construction noise but fines church music, that is likely unconstitutional. No clean SCOTUS ruling specifically on church worship noise.

Key Cases
Employment Division v. Smith (1990) RLUIPA (2000) Reed v. Town of Gilbert (2015)
Q17
Can the state close a church-run juvenile home for spanking?
Largely Resolved — Generally Yes, But Patchwork
Federal Status

Prince v. Massachusetts (SCOTUS, 1944) — state parens patriae power can override religious freedom to protect children. Fulton v. Philadelphia (2021) — if licensing has discretionary exemptions, denying one to a religious home triggers strict scrutiny.

Q18
Can religious solicitation be confined to official booths?
Resolved — Depends on Forum Type
Federal Status

Heffron v. ISKCON (SCOTUS, 1981) — YES at state fairs (booth restrictions are valid). ISKCON v. Lee (SCOTUS, 1992) — YES for solicitation at airports, NO for literature distribution at airports. On public streets and sidewalks — NO, would fail strict scrutiny.

Key Cases
Heffron v. ISKCON (1981) ISKCON v. Lee (1992)
Q19
Is an unborn fetus a "person" entitled to Constitutional protection?
In Flux — Most Actively Contested
Federal Status

Roe v. Wade (1973) said no. Dobbs v. Jackson (2022) overturned Roe but DECLINED to rule on personhood. LePage v. Center for Reproductive Medicine (Alabama, 2024) — frozen embryos are "children" under state law. H.R. 722 (Life at Conception Act) is pending with 68+ co-sponsors but has approximately 1% chance of passage.

Q20
Can the Ten Commandments be posted in a public classroom?
In Flux — Actively Re-Litigated
Federal Status

Stone v. Graham (SCOTUS, 1980) said no — still technically binding precedent. Kennedy v. Bremerton (2022) killed the Lemon test that Stone relied on. A SCOTUS showdown is expected within 1–3 years.

Q21
Can students have a period of silent meditation and prayer?
Resolved — Yes, If Facially Neutral
Federal Status

Wallace v. Jaffree (SCOTUS, 1985) struck down Alabama's law for having religious purpose, but did NOT strike down neutral "meditation only" statutes. Post-Kennedy (2022), the historical-practices framework makes neutral moment-of-silence laws even more permissible.

Q22
Can Christmas carols be sung in public schools?
Resolved — Yes, As Education
Federal Status

Florey v. Sioux Falls (8th Cir., 1980) — religious music is permissible as cultural and educational heritage. Bauchman v. West High School (10th Cir., 1997) upheld a choir performing religious music for educational purposes.

A winter concert featuring "Silent Night" alongside "Jingle Bells" = constitutionally fine. An exclusively devotional religious service = not fine.

Key Cases
Florey v. Sioux Falls (8th Cir. 1980) Bauchman v. West High School (10th Cir. 1997)
Q23
Must an employer allow Saturday Sabbath worship?
Resolved — Strongly Yes
Federal Status

TWA v. Hardison (1977) gutted accommodation for 46 years — the "de minimis cost" standard allowed employers to deny requests. Groff v. DeJoy (SCOTUS, 2023, UNANIMOUS 9-0) overruled Hardison. Employers must now bear "substantial increased costs" before denying religious accommodation. Co-worker complaints alone are insufficient justification. One of the most dramatic reversals on this list.

Key Cases
Groff v. DeJoy, 600 U.S. 447 (2023) TWA v. Hardison (1977) — overruled
Q24
Can a high school graduation be held in a church?
In Flux — Genuinely Unsettled
Federal Status

Elmbrook School District v. Doe (7th Cir. en banc, 2012) said no — holding a graduation in a church with a pervasive religious environment violates the Establishment Clause. SCOTUS declined certiorari; Justices Scalia and Thomas dissented from that denial. Elmbrook is only binding in the 7th Circuit.

Kennedy v. Bremerton (2022) undermined Elmbrook's foundational Lemon test analysis. Under the historical-practices framework, civic events routinely held in churches throughout early American history would likely be treated very differently today.

Key Cases
Elmbrook v. Doe (7th Cir. 2012) Kennedy v. Bremerton (2022)
Q25
Can a state official seize a church and run it through a receiver?
Resolved — Clearly No
Federal Status

The most clearly resolved question on this list. Government cannot take over church governance — this violates both Religion Clauses plus the Ecclesiastical Abstention Doctrine. Established by Serbian Eastern Orthodox Diocese v. Milivojevich (SCOTUS, 1976), reinforced by Hosanna-Tabor (2012, unanimous) and Our Lady of Guadalupe (2020).

Key Cases
Serbian Orthodox v. Milivojevich (1976) Hosanna-Tabor v. EEOC (2012) Our Lady of Guadalupe (2020)
Q26
Can the state set minimum standards for religious school curricula?
Largely Resolved — Yes, With Limits
Federal Status

Pierce v. Society of Sisters (SCOTUS, 1925) — states may "reasonably regulate" private schools but cannot destroy the right of private choice in education. Wisconsin v. Yoder (1972) — Free Exercise can override state requirements.

Q27
Is religious tax exemption a right or privilege?
Resolved — Permitted, Not Required
Federal Status

Walz v. Tax Commission (SCOTUS, 1970, 8-1) — exemptions are constitutionally permitted; taxing churches would ironically create MORE government entanglement with religion, not less. Texas Monthly v. Bullock (1989) — exemptions must benefit a broad class, not religion only. Bob Jones University v. US (1983) — exemptions can be conditioned on not violating fundamental public policy.

Key Cases
Walz v. Tax Commission (1970) Texas Monthly v. Bullock (1989) Bob Jones University v. US (1983)
Q28
Should churches be taxed like any other part of society?
Resolved — No
Federal Status

All 50 states exempt church property from property taxes. Churches are automatically exempt from federal income tax under IRC §501(c)(3) without even filing an application. Churches are also exempt from Form 990 filing requirements. The clergy housing allowance was upheld in Gaylor v. Mnuchin (7th Cir., 2019). No pending legislation or serious challenges exist.

Key Cases
Walz v. Tax Commission (1970) IRC §501(c)(3) Gaylor v. Mnuchin (7th Cir. 2019)
Q29
Can federal labor laws enforce unionization in religious enterprises?
Resolved — No
Federal Status

NLRB v. Catholic Bishop of Chicago (SCOTUS, 1979, 5-4) — the NLRA does not extend jurisdiction to church-operated schools. Bethany College (NLRB, 2020) broadened this: the NLRB now declines jurisdiction over any religious, nonprofit institution. Recent applications: Loyola Marymount (2025), Saint Leo, and Saint Xavier all dissolved faculty unions citing this exemption.

Key Cases
NLRB v. Catholic Bishop of Chicago (1979) Bethany College (NLRB 2020)
Q30
Can the state require a license before a ministry solicits funds?
Resolved — Generally No
Federal Status

Watchtower Bible v. Village of Stratton (SCOTUS, 2002, 8-1) — permit requirements for canvassing violate the First Amendment. Cantwell v. Connecticut (1940) — licensing for religious solicitation is unconstitutional prior restraint on speech. Houses of worship are exempt from charitable solicitation registration requirements in every state.

Key Cases
Watchtower Bible v. Village of Stratton (2002) Cantwell v. Connecticut (1940)
Q31
Are religious hospitals, schools, counseling groups, etc. subject to government regulation?
Resolved — Yes, But With Expanding Protections
Federal Status

Religious institutions are subject to neutral health and safety regulations, but a series of landmark rulings has dramatically expanded religious institutional autonomy: RFRA (1993), RLUIPA (2000), Hobby Lobby (2014), Little Sisters of the Poor (2020), Fulton v. Philadelphia (2021), and Carson v. Makin (2022) have all reinforced that religious organizations cannot be excluded from government programs based on their religious character or identity.

Key Cases
RFRA (1993) RLUIPA (2000) Hobby Lobby (2014) Fulton v. Philadelphia (2021) Carson v. Makin (2022)

2000 RLUIPA — Religious Land Use and Institutionalized Persons Act enacted (Q7, Q13, Q16)
2012 Hosanna-Tabor v. EEOC — Ministerial exception recognized unanimously (Q10, Q11, Q15)
2019 American Legion v. AHA — Presumption of constitutionality for longstanding religious displays (Q12)

2022 Dobbs v. Jackson — Roe v. Wade overturned, personhood question returned to states (Q19) · Kennedy v. Bremerton — Lemon test eliminated (Q4, Q12, Q20, Q21)
