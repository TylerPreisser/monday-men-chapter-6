1

Is a minister of the gospel liable for malpractice for using spiritual guidance rather than psychological or medical techniques?

Resolved — No Liability
Federal Status

No court in America has recognized clergy malpractice as a valid cause of action — a recognition that the state lacks competence to define what constitutes faithful pastoral care. Nally v. Grace Community Church (Cal. Supreme Court, 1988) ruled unanimously that imposing a secular standard of care on pastoral counseling would violate the Establishment Clause. Over 7,000 churches filed amicus briefs — a striking picture of the church speaking with one voice on the autonomy of its ministry.

⚠ If a pastor holds a separate professional license (LPC, psychologist), they can be held to that profession's standard of care. The distinction matters: the state may regulate the psychologist's office, but it may not enter the pastor's study.
Key Cases
Nally v. Grace Community Church, 47 Cal.3d 278 (1988)
Sources
Nally v. Grace Community Church (1988) Church Law & Tax NC Central Law Review
State-by-State

No state in America has recognized clergy malpractice as a cause of action. This is not because of a single federal ruling — it is a consensus across all 50 states. The California Supreme Court's Nally decision (1988) is the leading case, but courts in Texas, Colorado, Illinois, and others have independently reached the same conclusion. The First Amendment creates a floor that no state can go below, and none has tried.

2

Can a Christian residence house in a college have the same standing as a fraternity/sorority for off-campus residency rules?

Largely Resolved — Yes in Principle
Federal Status

No single landmark case governs here, but the viewpoint discrimination doctrine strongly favors equal treatment. The principle is straightforward: if a university recognizes a fraternity house, it cannot deny the same standing to a Christian residence simply because its organizing conviction is theological rather than social.

Key precedents: Widmar v. Vincent (1981), Rosenberger v. UVA (1995), and Good News Club v. Milford (2001) establish that viewpoint-neutral access to facilities must include religious organizations.

⚠ Outcome is fact-specific — the protection is strongest at public institutions, where the First Amendment binds directly.
Key Cases
Widmar v. Vincent, 454 U.S. 263 (1981) Rosenberger v. UVA, 515 U.S. 819 (1995) Good News Club v. Milford (2001)
State-by-State

This is resolved by federal constitutional principles (viewpoint discrimination), which bind all public universities in every state. Private universities are not bound by the First Amendment and may set their own policies. States with state-level religious liberty statutes (21 states with state RFRAs) provide an additional layer of protection.

3

Can Christian high school students assemble on campus for religious discussion?

Resolved — Yes
Federal Status

Congress answered this question decisively with the Equal Access Act of 1984 — students who gather around Scripture have the same right to meet as any chess club or debate team. Board of Education v. Mergens (SCOTUS, 1990, 8-1) upheld the Act by an overwhelming margin. Good News Club v. Milford (SCOTUS, 2001, 6-3) extended the same protection down to elementary schools. Schools must annually certify compliance.

Key Cases
Equal Access Act of 1984 Board of Educ. v. Mergens, 496 U.S. 226 (1990) Good News Club v. Milford, 533 U.S. 98 (2001)
State-by-State

The Equal Access Act is federal law — it applies uniformly to every public secondary school in every state that receives federal funding (virtually all of them). No state can override it. The DOE requires annual certification of compliance from every school district.

4

Can Christian teachers in public schools meet before class for prayer?

Resolved — Yes, With Boundaries
Federal Status

In Kennedy v. Bremerton (SCOTUS, 2022, 6-3), the Court affirmed what should have been obvious: a man does not surrender his right to pray simply because he draws a government paycheck. The ruling overruled the Lemon test — an analytical framework that had chilled religious expression for decades. 2025 DOE guidance now explicitly states teachers may pray during lunch or meet for prayer before school.

⚠ The boundary remains where it should: teachers cannot use their official authority to coerce students into religious exercise. But private devotion among colleagues is exactly what the Free Exercise Clause was designed to protect.
Key Cases
Kennedy v. Bremerton, 597 U.S. 507 (2022) DOE Guidance (2025)
State-by-State

Kennedy v. Bremerton is binding federal precedent that applies in all 50 states. However, enforcement varies by school district. Some districts (particularly in the Northeast and Pacific states) maintain policies that are more restrictive than what Kennedy allows. Teachers in those districts may face administrative pushback even when the law is on their side. Organizations like First Liberty Institute and ADF handle these cases when they arise.

5

Can Christian college students meet in groups on state university campus?

Resolved — Yes
Federal Status

Widmar v. Vincent (SCOTUS, 1981, 8-1) established the right — decided the same year Schaeffer was writing these questions. Rosenberger v. UVA (1995) extended the protection to funding: if the university funds student publications, it cannot defund the ones that speak from a Christian perspective. InterVarsity v. University of Iowa (8th Cir., 2021) held that selective enforcement against religious groups is unconstitutional — and the officials responsible were held personally liable.

⚠ Real tension persists: CLS v. Martinez (2010) allows “all-comers” policies — but when those policies are enforced only against Christian groups while secular organizations maintain their membership standards, the pretense of neutrality collapses.
Key Cases
Widmar v. Vincent, 454 U.S. 263 (1981) Rosenberger v. UVA, 515 U.S. 819 (1995) InterVarsity v. Univ. of Iowa (8th Cir. 2021) CLS v. Martinez (2010)
State-by-State

Widmar v. Vincent applies to all public universities nationwide. The active tension point is the “all-comers” policy question — some state university systems (notably California State University, which deregistered InterVarsity chapters across all 23 campuses) use nondiscrimination policies to pressure religious groups. The 8th Circuit (InterVarsity v. Iowa, 2021) has pushed back hard. Circuit-by-circuit variation exists.

6

Can HEW require a Bible college to admit drug addicts/alcoholics as “handicapped persons”?

Resolved — Depends on Federal Funding
Federal Status

No federal funds: Neither Section 504 nor the ADA applies. Congress wisely recognized that religious organizations have their own authority structures — the ADA explicitly exempts them (42 U.S.C. §12187).

Any federal funds (including student aid) = Section 504 applies institution-wide per the Civil Rights Restoration Act of 1987. The trade-off is clear: accepting Caesar's money comes with Caesar's conditions. Current illegal drug users are excluded from protection (1992 amendments). Recovering addicts and alcoholics remain protected.

Key Cases
Section 504, Rehabilitation Act ADA Title III §12187 Civil Rights Restoration Act 1987 Grove City College v. Bell (1984)
State-by-State

The ADA's religious organization exemption (42 U.S.C. 12187) is federal and applies uniformly. Section 504 applies uniformly wherever federal funds are involved. There is no meaningful state-by-state variation on this question — the determining factor is federal funding, not geography.

7

Can a church build a religious school or day-care in a residential zone?

Resolved — Generally Yes
Federal Status

RLUIPA (2000) is one of the strongest tools Congress has given the church against local government overreach — providing strict scrutiny protection against zoning exclusions. A church cannot be singled out for exclusion where secular equivalents are permitted. Cutter v. Wilkinson (SCOTUS, 2005, unanimous) upheld RLUIPA. The DOJ enforces through its “Place to Worship Initiative.”

Key Cases
RLUIPA, 42 U.S.C. §2000cc (2000) Cutter v. Wilkinson, 544 U.S. 709 (2005)
State-by-State

RLUIPA is federal and applies in every state, but zoning is enforced locally — which means the practical experience varies enormously. The DOJ's “Place to Worship Initiative” has brought enforcement actions against municipalities in New York, New Jersey, California, Connecticut, Illinois, and many other states. Churches in smaller towns with less legal sophistication on their zoning boards tend to face more obstruction. The legal right is clear; the fight is local.

Can parents be prosecuted for sending kids to unapproved religious schools?

Largely Resolved — Generally No
Federal Status

Wisconsin v. Yoder (SCOTUS, 1972) established a principle Schaeffer would have appreciated: the state's interest in education does not extinguish the prior claim of parents to raise their children according to conscience. All 50 states now permit homeschooling and private religious schooling in some form — a dramatic shift from the landscape of the early 1980s.

9

Is an independent religious school exempt from unemployment taxes like church-owned schools?

Resolved Against — No
Federal Status

St. Martin Evangelical Lutheran Church v. South Dakota (SCOTUS, 1981) — the FUTA exemption applies only to schools legally part of a church or “operated, supervised, controlled, or principally supported by” a church. Independent religious schools must pay unemployment taxes — however sincere their Christian mission — and this remains the one question on this list where the law clearly resolved against the religious institution. Congress has not amended the statute in the decades since.

Key Cases
St. Martin v. South Dakota, 451 U.S. 772 (1981) DOL UIPL 28-87
State-by-State

St. Martin v. South Dakota is binding federal precedent under FUTA — the exemption framework is the same in every state. States administer unemployment insurance differently, but the church/independent school distinction applies everywhere. No state-level variation on this question.

10

Will the State enforce anti-discrimination laws against a church that fires a homosexual staff member?

In Flux — Partially Resolved
Federal Status

For ministers/religious function employees: The state cannot intervene — the ministerial exception recognizes that the church, not the government, decides who carries out its mission (Hosanna-Tabor 2012, unanimous; Our Lady of Guadalupe 2020, 7-2).

For non-ministerial employees: This remains the sharpest edge of the church-state question in 2026 — whether a congregation can hold its own members to the standards of its own confession. Bostock v. Clayton County (2020) extended Title VII to sexual orientation but left open RFRA and Section 702 exemptions. Braidwood Management v. EEOC (5th Cir., 2023) held RFRA requires an exemption. SCOTUS has NOT directly ruled on the Bostock/RFRA intersection.

11

Can seminary trustees refuse to graduate a practicing homosexual?

Resolved — Yes
Federal Status

A seminary exists to form ministers of the gospel — and the law, on this point, has recognized that the church must be free to determine who is fit for that calling. Four independent legal shields: (1) Ministerial exception (Hosanna-Tabor, Our Lady of Guadalupe). (2) Title VII Section 702 religious organization exemption. (3) Title IX religious exemption — upheld in Hunter v. DOE (9th Cir., 2024). (4) RFRA. The Fuller Seminary litigation (9th Cir., 2021) dismissed all claims after expulsion for same-sex marriage.

Key Cases
Hosanna-Tabor v. EEOC (2012) Our Lady of Guadalupe (2020) Hunter v. DOE (9th Cir. 2024)
State-by-State

The ministerial exception is a constitutional doctrine — it applies in every state and no state law can override it. Title IX's religious exemption is likewise federal. The real question is whether a state attorney general in a progressive state would bring an enforcement action and force a seminary to litigate. Seminaries in states like California, New York, and Massachusetts face a more adversarial enforcement environment than those in Texas, Tennessee, or Mississippi — even though the legal protections are the same.

12

Can a city continue its nativity scene in front of city hall?

Resolved — Almost Certainly Yes
Federal Status

Lynch v. Donnelly (SCOTUS, 1984) — nativity scene OK as part of a broader holiday display. American Legion v. AHA (SCOTUS, 2019, 7-2) established a “presumption of constitutionality” for longstanding displays, recognizing that religious symbols woven into civic life are part of the fabric of the nation, not violations of it. Kennedy v. Bremerton (2022) eliminated the Lemon test. Under the “historical practices” framework, a 40-year tradition is extremely strong.

Key Cases
Lynch v. Donnelly (1984) American Legion v. AHA (2019) Kennedy v. Bremerton (2022)
State-by-State

This is a local government decision, and practice varies enormously. Cities in the South and Midwest routinely maintain nativity displays with little controversy. Cities in the Northeast and on the West Coast have faced more legal challenges and many have voluntarily removed displays to avoid litigation — even though the legal framework now strongly favors them post-Kennedy (2022). The ACLU and Freedom From Religion Foundation remain active in filing complaints. Whether a city maintains its display often depends more on the courage of its city council than on the law.

13

Can zoning laws prevent home Bible studies?

Largely Resolved — No, But Friction Persists
Federal Status

RLUIPA (2000) — if a neighborhood can host poker nights and book clubs without a permit, it cannot single out the gathering that opens with prayer. Manhattan Beach, CA (2024) was forced to back down after ACLJ intervention.

⚠ The law is clear; the problem is on the ground. Local code enforcement officers often ignore or are simply unaware of federal protections — which means Christians gathering in homes must sometimes be prepared to assert rights that are already theirs.
Key Cases
RLUIPA (2000) Cutter v. Wilkinson (2005) ACLJ Manhattan Beach (2024)
State-by-State

RLUIPA is federal, but zoning enforcement is hyper-local. Documented enforcement actions against home Bible studies have occurred in California (Manhattan Beach 2024, San Juan Capistrano 2012), Arizona (Gilbert 2009), and various other municipalities. Every documented case has ultimately been resolved in favor of the homeowner, but the harassment itself is the punishment — homeowners must hire lawyers and wait months for resolution. States with state RFRAs provide an additional layer of protection.

Can a court decide which group gets the sanctuary in a church split?

Largely Resolved — Property Yes, Doctrine No
Federal Status

Serbian Eastern Orthodox Diocese v. Milivojevich (SCOTUS, 1976) — civil courts have no competence to adjudicate matters of doctrine and church governance. Jones v. Wolf (SCOTUS, 1979) — courts CAN use “neutral principles” (deeds, corporate charters) to resolve property disputes without entangling in doctrine.

⚠ Active context: ~7,000 United Methodist churches disaffiliated 2019–2024 over doctrinal convictions. Litigation ongoing. Alabama Supreme Court (2025) rejected the UMC trust clause — a significant development for congregations that believe faithfulness to Scripture required them to leave.

15

Must a religious school hire a practicing homosexual as a teacher?

Resolved — No
Federal Status

The law here is clear, and its reasoning sound: a religious school cannot faithfully transmit its convictions if the state dictates who stands in its classrooms. Three independent shields: (1) Ministerial exception — covers nearly all teachers at religious schools after Our Lady of Guadalupe (2020). (2) Title VII Section 702 religious organization exemption. (3) RFRA. Billard v. Charlotte Catholic (4th Cir., 2024) dismissed a teacher’s claim after firing for announcing a same-sex engagement.

Key Cases
Hosanna-Tabor v. EEOC (2012) Our Lady of Guadalupe (2020) Billard v. Charlotte Catholic (4th Cir. 2024)
State-by-State

The ministerial exception is a constitutional floor in all 50 states. However, the scope of state-level SOGI employment laws creates real variation in litigation risk for non-ministerial employees. In states like Mississippi, Texas, and Alabama (strong state RFRA, no SOGI statute), the risk is negligible. In states like California, New York, and Colorado (strong SOGI protections, narrow religious exemptions, aggressive enforcement), even a well-protected religious school may face the burden of litigation before prevailing. See Q10 for the full state-by-state breakdown of religious employer protections.

16

Can a church be fined for exuberant noise in worship?

Largely Resolved — Nuanced
Federal Status

A truly neutral noise ordinance CAN apply (Employment Division v. Smith, 1990). But RLUIPA (2000) provides strict scrutiny if the ordinance substantially burdens religious practice or discriminates. Reed v. Town of Gilbert (2015) — content-based distinctions receive strict scrutiny.

If a city allows construction noise but fines church music, that is likely unconstitutional. No clean SCOTUS ruling specifically on church worship noise.

Key Cases
Employment Division v. Smith (1990) RLUIPA (2000) Reed v. Town of Gilbert (2015)
State-by-State

Noise regulation is entirely local — ordinances vary by city and county. RLUIPA provides a federal floor, but enforcement depends on local government. Churches in urban areas face more complaints and enforcement actions than those in suburban or rural settings. There is no comprehensive state-by-state data because the variation is at the municipal level, not the state level.

18

Can religious solicitation be confined to official booths?

Resolved — Depends on Forum Type
Federal Status

Heffron v. ISKCON (SCOTUS, 1981) — YES at state fairs (booth restrictions are valid). ISKCON v. Lee (SCOTUS, 1992) — YES for solicitation at airports, NO for literature distribution at airports. On public streets and sidewalks — the traditional forums where the gospel has been proclaimed since the Apostles — booth-only restrictions would fail strict scrutiny.

Key Cases
Heffron v. ISKCON (1981) ISKCON v. Lee (1992)
State-by-State

The Supreme Court rulings (Heffron, ISKCON v. Lee) set a federal framework that applies in all states. The variable is the type of venue, not the state. State fairs, airports, shopping centers, and public streets follow the same constitutional framework everywhere. Some municipalities have more aggressive solicitation ordinances than others, but churches and religious organizations are exempt from charitable solicitation registration in every state.

Is an unborn fetus a “person” entitled to Constitutional protection?

In Flux — Most Actively Contested
Federal Status

Roe v. Wade (1973) said no — and for nearly fifty years, that answer foreclosed the deeper question. Dobbs v. Jackson (2022) overturned Roe but DECLINED to rule on personhood. The door Schaeffer wanted opened is now open, but the church still has the harder work of persuasion ahead. LePage v. Center for Reproductive Medicine (Alabama, 2024) — frozen embryos are “children” under state law. H.R. 722 (Life at Conception Act) is pending with 68+ co-sponsors but has approximately 1% chance of passage.

Can students have a period of silent meditation and prayer?

Resolved — Yes, If Facially Neutral
Federal Status

Wallace v. Jaffree (SCOTUS, 1985) struck down Alabama’s law — not because silence before God is unconstitutional, but because the legislature's stated purpose was transparently religious. Neutral “meditation only” statutes were left untouched. Post-Kennedy (2022), the historical-practices framework makes neutral moment-of-silence laws even more permissible. A student who chooses to spend that moment in prayer is exercising a right the Constitution was written to protect.

22

Can Christmas carols be sung in public schools?

Resolved — Yes, As Education
Federal Status

Florey v. Sioux Falls (8th Cir., 1980) — recognized what should be self-evident: the sacred music tradition is part of Western civilization's inheritance, and studying it is education, not indoctrination. Bauchman v. West High School (10th Cir., 1997) upheld a choir performing religious music for educational purposes.

A winter concert featuring “Silent Night” alongside “Jingle Bells” = constitutionally fine. An exclusively devotional religious service = not fine.

Key Cases
Florey v. Sioux Falls (8th Cir. 1980) Bauchman v. West High School (10th Cir. 1997)
State-by-State

This is resolved by federal circuit court precedent and applies in all states. The practical variation is at the school district level — some principals and superintendents are more cautious than the law requires. Schools in the South and Midwest tend to include more religious music in holiday programs. Schools in the Northeast and West Coast tend to be more cautious. But the legal right to include religious music as part of an educational program is the same everywhere.

23

Must an employer allow Saturday Sabbath worship?

Resolved — Strongly Yes
Federal Status

For 46 years, TWA v. Hardison (1977) made Sabbath observance nearly impossible for many workers — the “de minimis cost” standard allowed employers to deny requests with almost no justification. Groff v. DeJoy (SCOTUS, 2023, UNANIMOUS 9-0) overruled Hardison. Employers must now bear “substantial increased costs” before denying religious accommodation. Co-worker complaints alone are insufficient justification. The unanimous decision signals that the Court recognizes the Sabbath claim as something weightier than an employee preference.

Key Cases
Groff v. DeJoy, 600 U.S. 447 (2023) TWA v. Hardison (1977) — overruled
State-by-State

Groff v. DeJoy is a unanimous Supreme Court interpretation of Title VII — it applies to every employer with 15+ employees in every state. There is no state-by-state variation on the core right. Some states (like New York, California, Connecticut) have state employment laws that provide additional religious accommodation protections beyond federal Title VII. No state provides less protection than the federal Groff standard.

24

Can a high school graduation be held in a church?

In Flux — Genuinely Unsettled
Federal Status

Elmbrook School District v. Doe (7th Cir. en banc, 2012) said no — holding a graduation in a church with a pervasive religious environment violates the Establishment Clause. SCOTUS declined certiorari; Justices Scalia and Thomas dissented from that denial. Elmbrook is only binding in the 7th Circuit.

Kennedy v. Bremerton (2022) undermined Elmbrook’s foundational Lemon test analysis. Under the historical-practices framework, civic events routinely held in churches throughout early American history would likely be treated very differently today.

Key Cases
Elmbrook v. Doe (7th Cir. 2012) Kennedy v. Bremerton (2022)
State-by-State

Elmbrook v. Doe (7th Circuit, 2012) is only binding in Wisconsin, Illinois, and Indiana. No other circuit has ruled on this question. In all other states, there is no appellate precedent prohibiting the practice. In practice, many school districts in rural areas across the South and Midwest hold graduation ceremonies in church buildings — the largest air-conditioned venue in town — without legal challenge.

25

Can a state official seize a church and run it through a receiver?

Resolved — Clearly No
Federal Status

The most clearly resolved question on this list. Government cannot take over church governance — this violates both Religion Clauses plus the Ecclesiastical Abstention Doctrine. Christ is the head of the church, and the state has no authority to appoint a substitute. Established by Serbian Eastern Orthodox Diocese v. Milivojevich (SCOTUS, 1976), reinforced by Hosanna-Tabor (2012, unanimous) and Our Lady of Guadalupe (2020).

Key Cases
Serbian Orthodox v. Milivojevich (1976) Hosanna-Tabor v. EEOC (2012) Our Lady of Guadalupe (2020)
State-by-State

This is resolved by federal constitutional law (First Amendment) and applies uniformly in all 50 states. No state can seize a church and appoint a receiver. Some states have modified their nonprofit receivership statutes to explicitly exempt religious organizations. The protection is absolute and geography-independent.

27

Is religious tax exemption a right or privilege?

Resolved — Permitted, Not Required
Federal Status

Walz v. Tax Commission (SCOTUS, 1970, 8-1) — exemptions are constitutionally permitted; taxing churches would ironically create MORE government entanglement with religion, not less. The power to tax is the power to destroy, and keeping the state's hand off the church's finances serves both institutions. Texas Monthly v. Bullock (1989) — exemptions must benefit a broad class, not religion only. Bob Jones University v. US (1983) — exemptions can be conditioned on not violating fundamental public policy.

Key Cases
Walz v. Tax Commission (1970) Texas Monthly v. Bullock (1989) Bob Jones University v. US (1983)
State-by-State

Walz v. Tax Commission is binding federal precedent. All 50 states independently exempt church property from property tax — this is both a constitutional permission and a universal state legislative choice. The variation is in scope: some states exempt only the sanctuary, while others exempt parsonages, parking lots, and ancillary buildings. States like Texas and Florida have very broad exemptions. States like New York and California have narrower definitions of exempt property.

28

Should churches be taxed like any other part of society?

Resolved — No
Federal Status

All 50 states exempt church property from property taxes — a recognition stretching back to the founding that the church serves a purpose the state cannot replicate and should not burden. Churches are automatically exempt from federal income tax under IRC §501(c)(3) without even filing an application. Churches are also exempt from Form 990 filing requirements, providing protections against the kind of government surveillance of religious activity that the Founders feared. The clergy housing allowance was upheld in Gaylor v. Mnuchin (7th Cir., 2019). No pending legislation or serious challenges exist.

Key Cases
Walz v. Tax Commission (1970) IRC §501(c)(3) Gaylor v. Mnuchin (7th Cir. 2019)
State-by-State

All 50 states exempt churches from property tax. Federal income tax exemption under IRC 501(c)(3) applies nationally. There is no state-by-state variation on the core question — it is uniform across the entire country. The only variation is in the scope of property covered (see Q27).

Can federal labor laws enforce unionization in religious enterprises?

Resolved — No
Federal Status

NLRB v. Catholic Bishop of Chicago (SCOTUS, 1979, 5-4) — the NLRA does not extend jurisdiction to church-operated schools, recognizing that inserting a federal labor board into the governance of a religious institution would raise grave constitutional concerns. Bethany College (NLRB, 2020) broadened this: the NLRB now declines jurisdiction over any religious, nonprofit institution. Recent applications: Loyola Marymount (2025), Saint Leo, and Saint Xavier all dissolved faculty unions citing this exemption.

Key Cases
NLRB v. Catholic Bishop of Chicago (1979) Bethany College (NLRB 2020)
State-by-State

NLRB v. Catholic Bishop is federal and applies everywhere. The NLRB's Bethany College standard (declining jurisdiction over religious nonprofits) is applied nationally. Some states have their own state labor boards, but state courts have generally followed the federal framework in exempting religious institutions. No meaningful state-by-state variation.

Can the state require a license before a ministry solicits funds?

Resolved — Generally No
Federal Status

Watchtower Bible v. Village of Stratton (SCOTUS, 2002, 8-1) — permit requirements for canvassing violate the First Amendment, affirming that the gospel does not need a government license to be shared. Cantwell v. Connecticut (1940) — licensing for religious solicitation is unconstitutional prior restraint on speech. Houses of worship are exempt from charitable solicitation registration requirements in every state.

Key Cases
Watchtower Bible v. Village of Stratton (2002) Cantwell v. Connecticut (1940)
State-by-State

Watchtower v. Stratton and Cantwell v. Connecticut are binding federal precedent. Houses of worship are exempt from charitable solicitation registration in every state. The variation is for non-church religious ministries (parachurch organizations, evangelistic associations) — some states exempt them and some require registration. But the core right to share the gospel door-to-door without a government permit is uniform nationwide.

Are religious hospitals, schools, counseling groups, etc. subject to government regulation?

Resolved — Yes, But With Expanding Protections
Federal Status

Religious institutions are subject to neutral health and safety regulations, but a series of landmark rulings has dramatically expanded religious institutional autonomy — each affirming that the church serves a purpose the state cannot replicate. RFRA (1993), RLUIPA (2000), Hobby Lobby (2014), Little Sisters of the Poor (2020), Fulton v. Philadelphia (2021), and Carson v. Makin (2022) have all reinforced that religious organizations cannot be excluded from the public square simply because they are religious.

Key Cases
RFRA (1993) RLUIPA (2000) Hobby Lobby (2014) Fulton v. Philadelphia (2021) Carson v. Makin (2022)
State-by-State

The federal framework (RFRA, RLUIPA, ministerial exception) sets a floor, but regulation varies enormously by sector and state. Religious hospitals face different regulatory environments in California versus Texas. Religious adoption agencies have been forced out of government contracting in Massachusetts, Illinois, and D.C. but continue operating freely in most other states. Faith-based schools face widely different curriculum and licensing requirements (see Q26). The common thread: neutral health and safety regulation applies everywhere, but the scope of “nondiscrimination” requirements varies dramatically.
